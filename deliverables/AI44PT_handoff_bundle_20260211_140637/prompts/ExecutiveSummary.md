# The Environmental Policy Paradox: Why Sustainability Governance is Failing our Planet and What to Do About It (PART I: Executive Summary)

**The Puzzle**
*   This book offers an innovative explanation for an alarming environmental policy paradox that has vexed students and practitioners of sustainability policy and governance for over three decades: *accelerating policy attention* to ameliorating, alongside *alarming degradation of*, the *earth's biosphere*.
    *   The decline has been largely captured, ever since the 1992 Rio Earth Summit, through research and attention on *climate, biodiversity, and forests* as three interconnected "triplet" global environmental problems.
*   I show that existing theories for this paradox are either important but insufficient, or misplaced:
    *   *economic globalization hypothesis:*
        *   H: efforts to attract global capital has placed downward pressure on environmental standards and regulations
    *   *power hypothesis*
        *   H: individuals, organizations, and classes, including businesses and economic actors actively engage to shape, and constrain, environmental policy and regulations consistent with their interests
    *   *implementation hypothesis*
        *   H: weak policy design and enforcement capacities undermines an ability to implement desired environmental policy objectives

**The argument**
*   I argue that while the first two are useful, they are insufficient, while the third is misplaced.
    *   Globalization has certainly been used as a rationale for reducing environmental regulation in order to attract capital, but it, alone, cannot fully explain the paradox because we do see, in many cases, the adoption of effective environmental policies and regulations that did undermine investment opportunities.
    *   Power is most certainly relevant as a great number of masterful accounts have shown that operate through overt, covert and latent ways to shape or minimize environmental regulations and policies. However, this too, is incomplete since some jurisdictions and cases have developed strong environmental policies either in spite of, or owing to support from, powerful interests.
    *   The weak policy design capacity/weak implementation hypothesis is misplaced. In fact, believe that this explained the paradox has led to 35 years of concerted efforts to improve policy and governance capacity and financing for implementation, which. has only served to produce an exponential number of policy innovations that are, themselves, unable to ameliorate the environmental problems for which they were created. Instead, I show that these efforts are misplace as they unconsciously and effectively reinforce Type 3, 2 and 1 conceptions while drifting away from Type 4 policy design capacities.
*   A more complete understanding of this paradox is that policy analysts and advocates have, often unconsciously, generated "ill fit for purpose" problem conceptions that have made it impossible to conceive of, let alone deploy, interventions for effective environmental outcomes
*   I apply and elaborate this argument my "four problem types" (4PT) framework to advance a more nuanced and counterintuitive explanation: that ongoing degradation amidst policy attention is includes widespread cases of *policy effectiveness*.
*   I argue that failure to explicitly identify the type of problem a sustainability policy or governance solution is targeting has led to:
    *   the tragic production of effective, but "ill fit for purpose", Type 3, 2 and 1 policy interventions for managing Type 4 environmental crises
    *   corresponding reduction in Type 4 policy design capacities

**The Four Problem Types (4PT) Framework**
*   My 4PT framework, developed and applied with several colleagues, identifies not one, but *four types of problems*. These problem types are the product of two interconnected simultaneous processes: how problem focused scholars and practitioners see the world around them which, in turn, biases the kinds of empirical evidence they collect.
*   The four types are distinguished according to two questions (Table ES1):
    *   Does the analysis, conclusions, and theories treat individuals, organizations and states as largely self-interested, satisfaction driven entities that seek some kind "utility enhancing" outcome?
    *   Is the analysis, conclusions, and theories generated from, and narrowed to, understanding, improving, or solving, a clearly specified "on the ground" problem or class of problems?

*   This typology serves well to distinguish how analysts treat any on the ground problem. An accompanied code book has been designed to advance efforts into other realms including health, social services, and education.
*   When applied to the world of sustainability policy and governance four key schools of thought and practice have emerged that reinforce each.
    *   Commons (Type 1 reinforcing)
    *   Optimization (Type 2 reinforcing)
    *   Compromise (Type 3 reinforcing)
    *   Prioritization (Type 4 reinforcing)
*   These schools are characterized by key dimensions including:
    *   strong *ethical foundations* about the need to manage or solve their preferred problem type
    *   sophisticated *research methods* that implicitly lead researchers to collect evidence that *unconsciously biases* one problem Type over others
    *   clearly articulated *prescriptive projects*
*   **Unconscious bias:** research methods that serve each school's ethical foundations about what kinds of problems are most important, reproduce unconscious biases such that member of each school believes that their approach to generating empirical evidence is "neutral", rather than biasing one problem Type over the other. This helps explain three decades of drift away from conceiving of, and managing, Type 4 climate, biodiversity and forest challenges.
*   **Whack-a-mole effects:** I show that in contrast to prevailing normative assumptions and even declarative conclusions from some sustainability scientists that smart policy designs can realize synergies across most sustainability objectives – what I refer to as "birds", application of the 4PT framework shows *overwhelming scientific evidence that solving for one problem Type often exacerbates others* – what I refer to as "moles"
*   Overcoming unconscious bias requires that each member of these schools understands, and is transparent about, the conceptual frames that shape and narrow their research, methods, analyses, and policy recommendations.
*   This paves the way for returning to, and developing, policy design capacities for managing, rather than drifting away from, Type 4 problems.

**Empirical assessment**
*   The book applies this framework to make the following arguments:
    *   Sustainability scholars and practitioners have drifted away from conceiving of environmental challenges as Type 4 problems
    *   This drift shows up in the way:
        *   research is conducted
        *   policy recommendations are made
        *   professional environmental managers are trained to assess, and find solutions for, Type 4 problems
    *   This drifts subtly but powerfully helps account for 30 years of Type 4 drift in policy interventions to address climate, biodiversity and forest challenges
    *   Sustainability scholars and practitioners have agency to reverse course by developing Type 4 policy design capacities
*   These arguments are largely illustrated by applying a qualitative "historical narrative" approach that is best equipped to uncover complex historical phenomenon that have been missed by more narrow assessments.
*   These are supplemented with the results of quantitative coding of the 4PT framework on scientific publications, pedagogy and impact evaluations
    *   This was accomplished by producing a lengthy code book in parallel to this book that my research team and I developed and applied to categorizing qualitative text into one of the four types. Initial application of the code book shows great promise in a future empirical research agenda assessing the prominence of different types in research outcomes, policy documents, and policy tools.
*   The quantitative results are:
    *   consistent with, and corroborate evidence, from the historical narrative assessments; generate an exciting research agenda including:
        *   uncovering the conditions through which individuals are likely to find themselves in one of the schools
        *   the building of qualitative and large-N data assessments of today's government and private sector policy interventions

**Reversing course: managing Type 4 problems**
*   The empirical assessment shows *strong support for the hypothesis that successfully reversing years of "policy creation euphoria, implementation depression" cycles of ineffective policies for climate, biodiversity and forests management requires a fundamental shift* from treating the crisis as a Type 3 (Compromise), Type 2 (Optimization), or even Type 1 (Commons) problem, to conceiving it as a Type 4 (Prioritization) challenge.
*   The book goes further than documenting and reviewing empirical evidence for my argument. It also identifies how to overcome the drift away from Type 4 problems
*   This prescriptive section of the book identifies promising policy analysis techniques for reversing course:
    *   revision and expansion of my collaborative work on *path dependency analysis*
    *   identification of the promise of "thermostatic institutions" to manage Type 4 problems
    *   discussion of promising protocols and procedures for advancing Type 4 policy design learning and innovations
*   I show that doing so requires developing, and applying, the "integrative anticipatory policy design" (IAPD) framework that will assist policy analysts and advocates in:
    *   engaging sequentialist/lexical ordering processes
    *   identifying key features of the problem that any solution would need to incorporate to effectively overcome
    *   identify key features of a proposed intervention that shape its multiple step "causal influence" logics
    *   how to apply pathways analysis capable of producing, and nurturing, "critical juncture" trajectories that are strong enough to fend off pressures for policy conception drift
    *   how to organize multi-stakeholder policy design learning exercises that integrate complex sources of knowledge produced within, and across, the ecological and policy sciences

**A research agenda**
*   the book then identifies the need for a "Marshall plan" research agenda devoted to uncovering and applying path dependency analysis with which to:
    *   foster durable policy change and pathways for managing Type 4 climate, biodiversity and forest challenges
    *   research thermostatic institutions for design insights
