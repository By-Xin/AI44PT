# Elevated urban energy risks due to climate-driven biophysical feedbacks

Xinchang 'Cathy' Li, Lei Zhao, Yue Qin, Keith Oleson, Yiwen Zhang

Climate-driven impacts on future urban heating and cooling (H&C) energy demand are critical to sustainable energy planning. Existing global H&C projections are predominantly made without accounting for future two-way biophysical feedbacks between urban climate and H&C use. Here, using a hybrid modelling framework we show that the prevalent degree-days methods misrepresent the magnitude, nonlinearity and uncertainty in the climate-driven projections of H&C energy demand changes due to the missing two-way feedbacks. We find a 220% increase (47% decrease) in cooling (heating) energy demand with amplified uncertainty by 2099 under a very high emission scenario, roughly twice that projected by previous methods. The spatially diverse H&C demand responses to the warming climates highlight the disparate challenges faced by individual cities and necessitate urban energy planning accounting for local climate-energy interactions. Our study underscores the critical necessity of explicit and dynamic modelling of urban H&C energy use for climate-sensitive energy planning.

Buildings today consume ~20% of delivered energy of the world, >40% of which is used for space heating and cooling (H&C). The H&C is among the most sensitive of energy-use sectors to climate change and is therefore critical to energy projection, planning and security under future climate change.

The H&C energy use interacts with the climate system both biogeochemically and biophysically. Previous research has predominantly focused on the global-scale biogeochemical feedback between H&C energy use and future warmer climates. Warmer climates induce changes in net energy demand and global energy mix that influence GHG emissions, which in turn affects climate change pathways through the biogeochemical feedback.

Biophysical feedbacks, on the other hand, operate on local-to-regional scales and are less studied. Within urban areas, anthropogenic heat from H&C use to the urban canyon can be a substantial part of the local surface energy budget, altering urban microclimate and thereby influencing H&C energy demand. Warmer future climates may promote cooling use and release more anthropogenic heat into urban air, thus further warming the urban environment. This forms a self-reinforcing, positive feedback loop between building cooling use and the urban environment. On the other hand, rising temperatures under climate change could potentially decrease energy demand in heating seasons. Less heating use entails less anthropogenic heat release into the urban environment than without climate change, which induces less urban warming than under the present climate. This forms a negative biophysical feedback loop that may dampen the heating demand decrease. Such changes in H&C energy demand may polarize the seasonal electricity demand and shift the energy mixes. These biophysical feedbacks have critical implications in the understanding and projection of urban energy demand under climate change, its robustness and uncertainty, and local-to-global-scale energy planning.

Characterizing the effects of biophysical feedbacks under climate change poses methodological, scale and computational challenges for future H&C energy projections. Present methods for projecting H&C energy use under climate change can be broadly grouped into two categories: data-driven statistical methods and process-based dynamic modelling.

In statistical methods, empirical relationships are derived between observed past climate (primarily, air temperature) and H&C energy demand, electricity use, building end-use energy, sectorial end-use energy or primary energy consumption. These relationships are then applied to future climate projections to obtain future H&C energy demand/use projections. Among these studies, H&C degree-days (HDD and CDD) have been used prevalently to establish connections between the ambient climate and H&C energy use and to inform policy-making on large-scale energy risks and climate adaptations. Degree-days are cumulative daily outdoor temperatures above (for CDD) or below (for HDD) a base temperature that may require the use of space conditioning to ensure thermal comfort in buildings.

These data-driven approaches are computationally inexpensive for large-scale, multimodel and/or multiscenario applications but are subject to three critical limitations:

1. **Non-stationarity under climate change**: empirical models based on local and historical observations assume spatiotemporally invariant relationships to hold outside of the training range, which is questionable under decades of climate change because biophysical feedbacks will alter or amplify the mapping from weather to H&C energy use.
2. **Missing two-way feedbacks in degree-day proxies**: degree-day projections are calculated from Earth system model output in which urban climate modules are almost universally missing, let alone building energy parameterizations. These proxies thus reflect only the one-way effect of background climate on H&C energy and ignore the two-way interactions that form the biophysical feedback loops.
3. **Non-urban weather inputs**: weather stations are rarely located in real urban built environments, and traditional climate projections are essentially background, non-urban climate due to the near-universal lack of an urban representation in state-of-the-art ESMs. Urban climates exhibit unique spatiotemporal patterns and uncertainties; using background climates can misrepresent uncertainties and magnitude of change.

Recent efforts using dynamic modelling attempt to address some of these shortcomings. These methods use building energy models (BEMs) that are either driven by climatology or coupled with process-based models to study the impacts of climate on building energy use. Climatology-driven approaches cannot capture dynamic two-way interactions, while coupled approaches capture bidirectional interactions but are typically limited to small spatial scales and short time periods due to high computational demand, often relying on a single climate model and thus misrepresenting intermodel variability and uncertainty.

To characterize the previously overlooked yet critical biophysical feedbacks, we leverage a hybrid modelling framework that combines dynamic Earth system modelling and machine learning to study global urban H&C energy demand under urban climate change, variability and uncertainties. We use urban climate and building energy projections from the subgrid representation of urban microclimate processes (Community Land Model Urban, CLMU) integrated with an updated urban BEM in the Community Earth System Model v.2 (CESM2) to build urban climate and building energy emulators.

We validate the performance of the CLMU-BEM on reproducing global-scale H&C energy consumption against published global anthropogenic heat datasets, as well as the intensity of the biophysical feedbacks against numerous local-to-regional modelling and observational studies. We keep the urban extent, building stocks and H&C equipment type and ownership at their present-day levels throughout the CESM2 simulations to isolate climate-only effects (climate change and climate-driven biophysical feedbacks) on H&C energy demand.

The emulators are location-based neural network models that mimic the urban land-atmosphere coupling in CESM2 and thus learn the dynamic interactions between future H&C energy demand and realistic, globally coherent background future climate. We use 85 years (2015–2099) of monthly average atmospheric forcings and urban outputs from fully coupled CESM2 simulations to train and validate the emulators. The emulators are then applied to 20–24 other ESMs participating in CMIP6 over the same time range. Multimodel projections are made for monthly mean urban 2 m air temperature (Tu) and space heating (QH) and cooling (QC) energy demand fluxes with respect to urban areas under three SSP-RCP scenarios: SSP 2-4.5, 3-7.0 and 5-8.5.

## Strong contribution of biophysical feedbacks to H&C demand

The biophysical feedbacks result in distinct responses of H&C energy demand under climate-driven temperature increase. The simulated global multimodel mean urban warming (ΔTu) is 2–5 K by the end of the twenty-first century, depending on the scenario, along with which QC and QH are projected to change by 68% to 239% and −23% to −47%, respectively.

The paths along which QC and QH change in response to temperature change reveal the signatures of the biophysical feedbacks. Urban temperature increases lead to more diverging QC trends between scenarios, in which increase in QC accelerates towards the end of the century and under higher-emission scenarios. Such a phenomenon is not observed for QH, in which the decreases in QH appear mostly linear throughout the century. This is because the biophysical feedback between cooling use and urban temperature is positive, resulting in an accelerating increase in QC, whereas the biophysical feedback between heating use and urban climate is negative and exerts a damping effect on QH decrease between scenarios.

These divergent biophysical effects on QC and QH can also be seen in their intermodel variability. The positive QC–Tu feedback promotes divergence between models, hence the increase in multimodel uncertainty for QC towards the end of the century, whereas the negative QH–Tu feedback does not cause discernable changes in the intermodel variability for QH.

Failing to account for two-way feedbacks will misrepresent the magnitude, nonlinearity and uncertainty in ΔQC and ΔQH under climate change. We compare degree-day projections (CDD and HDD) from the IPCC Sixth Assessment Report with QC and QH projected by our method using the same set of CMIP6 models. Degree-day proxies substantially underestimate both increase in QC and decrease in QH under all three scenarios towards the end of the century.

Under SSP 5–8.5 (SSP 3–7.0, SSP 2–4.5):

- **Cooling**: QC increases by **220%** (139%, 61%), whereas CDD shows only a **100%** (71%, 36%) increase.
- **Heating**: QH decreases by **47%** (37%, 23%), whereas HDD shows only a **25%** (19%, 11%) decrease.

The underestimation stems from missing biophysical feedbacks in degree-day proxies: degree-days are calculated from ESMs lacking explicit urban schemes or building energy parameterizations, representing only the one-way effect from background climate to energy without capturing feedback effects of anthropogenic heat on urban climate.

Our ΔQC and ΔQH also show larger nonlinearity and much wider intermodel spread than ΔCDD and ΔHDD, respectively, under all three scenarios. The difference becomes more prominent under larger temperature changes in the second half of the century and under higher-emission scenarios. This indicates that the mapping from degree-days to H&C energy demand is not static; empirical relationships established in traditional statistical approaches will break down under larger temperature changes in higher-emission futures, invalidating long-run extrapolations.

## Increased intermodel uncertainty and spatial variability

Robust (high intermodel agreement) urban warming translates to substantially more uncertain and spatially variant H&C changes in future climates. The multimodel mean Tu increases by 3–8 K (1–4 K) across global urban areas under SSP 5-8.5 (SSP 2-4.5) by the last decade of the century. Warming generally becomes more pronounced towards higher latitudes and multimodel projections on ~72–74% of global urban areas are robust.

This relatively smooth spatial variation in Tu does not show in QC and QH. Under climate change effects alone, the increase in QC can vary by up to two orders of magnitude among regions. Hotspots of substantial growth in QC include coastal, Midwest and southeast United States, parts of Brazil, Eastern Europe, the Middle East, and central and south China. Some Global South regions show small changes in QC because cooling demand growth is predominantly limited by income under climate effects alone.

Multimodel agreement for QC projections is markedly lower than that for Tu (about 19–26% of urban areas). The hotspots and lowered agreement can be attributed to regionally unique biophysical feedbacks arising from diverse urban topologies, building properties and H&C ownership rates, as well as local meteorology.

The decrease in QH also varies by two orders of magnitude but follows Tu change patterns more closely. Models agree in decreases over ~47% of global urban areas where the most substantial decrease occurs (mid- to high-latitude regions with high present-day heating demand and substantial future warming).

These results illustrate that for H&C demand response to climate change, not only does the absolute temperature change matter, so do the pathways through which the temperature change occurs. This necessitates multimodel and/or multimember simulations for H&C demand that encompass several realizations of climate pathways. Cities need to prepare for a more uncertain energy future than previously projected; climate-conscious energy planning must consider added spatial variability and uncertainty from local climate-energy interactions.

## City-specific energy challenges under climate change

Comparing H&C responses of cities in a globally coherent modelling framework highlights unique challenges faced by different cities and elucidates differing mechanisms behind these challenges.

- Under similar climates and warming, cities may exhibit different H&C–temperature sensitivities due to differences in building materials and H&C ownership rates. Better building insulation and/or less H&C ownership lead to less temperature-sensitive H&C response.
- Cities with very low H&C ownership may show virtually no response to temperature change. Low ownership can be due to income and/or building types (for example, vernacular housing designed to promote natural ventilation).
- Cities with similar urban and building characteristics can face diverse challenges because of different background climates: warming may reduce heating-season energy in colder/temperate climates but increase stress on warmer cities via substantial and possibly uncertain cooling demand increases.
- Even with similar background climates and urban characteristics, cities can differ in multimodel uncertainty, mainly due to intermodel disagreement in projected climates and associated biophysical feedbacks.

Multimodel projections are essential for cities to understand possible energy futures and plan accordingly. Adaptation strategies should be tailored based on local information on the magnitude and uncertainty of a city’s H&C demand response.

## Discussion

Biophysical feedbacks between urban climate and H&C energy demand play a critical role in the uncertainty, nonlinearity and spatial variability of climate-driven H&C energy demand projections. Statistical relationships derived from past observations will break down under large temperature changes, making simple extrapolations problematic. Cities face diverse challenges due to unique biophysical feedbacks; climate-sensitive energy planning needs to prepare for more polarized H&C demand and a broader spectrum of energy futures than previously projected.

Multiscenario results suggest added benefits from climate mitigation in counteracting climate-driven cooling demand increase. These findings necessitate explicit and dynamic modelling of urban H&C energy demand that accounts for two-way interactions between H&C energy use and urban-specific climates. The biophysical feedbacks modelled here are probably conservative due to monthly-based analyses and omitted dehumidification energy in summer cooling.

This study investigates climatic drivers alone; climatic and non-climatic drivers will interactively shape future urban H&C energy demand. These complex drivers call for physics-based multisector modelling of urban energy and climate and continued improvement in physical representations of urban energy systems in large-scale models. Overlaying these projections with population and income growth can identify further regions of concern and guide city- or region-specific studies, supporting building adaptation and energy infrastructure planning under a warming climate.
