# Global patterns and drivers of tropical aboveground carbon changes

**Yu Feng, Philippe Ciais, Jean-Pierre Wigneron, Yidi Xu, Alan D. Ziegler, Dave van Wees, Arthur Nicolaus Fendrich, Dominick V. Spracklen, Stephen Sitch, Martin Brandt, Wei Li, Lei Fan, Xiaojun Li, Jie Wu, Zhenzhong Zeng**

Tropical terrestrial ecosystems play an important role in modulating the global carbon balance. However, the complex dynamics and factors controlling tropical aboveground live biomass carbon (AGC) are not fully understood. Here, using remotely sensed observations, we find a moderate net AGC sink of $0.21 \pm 0.06\,\mathrm{PgC\,yr^{-1}}$ throughout the global tropics from 2010 to 2020. This arises from a gross loss of $-1.79\,\mathrm{PgC\,yr^{-1}}$ offset by a marked gain of $2.01 \pm 0.06\,\mathrm{PgC\,yr^{-1}}$. Fire emissions in non-forested African shrubland/savanna biomes, coupled with post-fire carbon recovery, substantially dominated the interannual variability of tropical AGC. Fire radiative power was identified as the primary determinant of the spatial variability in AGC gains, with soil moisture also playing a crucial role in shaping trends. We highlight the dominant roles of anthropogenic and hydroclimatic determinants in orchestrating tropical land carbon dynamics and advocate for land management to conserve indispensable ecosystem services worldwide.

Tropical terrestrial ecosystems encompass more than half of the global aboveground live biomass carbon (AGC) and are critical components of the global carbon cycle. Even relatively minor changes in tropical AGC potentially have an important influence on global atmospheric $\mathrm{CO_2}$ concentration, subsequently modulating climate change. Contemporary estimates of tropical AGC changes often hinge on broad assumptions, sparse ground data and/or inaccurate estimation methods, leading to uncertainties in identifying tropical land carbon sources/sinks. Satellite observations have shown that deforestation and forest degradation cause tropical forest AGC to be net carbon sources, whereas others suggested that carbon sequestration by secondary forests resulted in tropical forests being net carbon sinks. A key research question for the tropical carbon budget is whether losses from current disturbances in some regions are offset by recovery from past disturbances in other regions. Another complicating issue is that studies usually focus on forests, ignoring tropical non-forested ecosystems that have a great influence on the trends and variability in the land carbon sink, with fire often playing a critical role in their AGC changes.

Current limited understanding of the tropical carbon budget stems from several factors, including limitations in optical satellite-based forest AGC quantification, inaccuracies of coarse satellite products that lead to erroneous quantification of forest disturbance and/or fire activities, and an incomplete understanding of climate and anthropogenic impacts on AGC changes. The AGC maps used in previous studies, despite their high spatial resolution (30–500 m), mostly provide static forest biomass or cannot quantify the trajectories of AGC across all terrestrial ecosystems. Developing robust and temporally consistent maps for quantifying AGC dynamics, accompanied by a comprehensive understanding of the controlling factors, is crucial in minimizing uncertainties in land carbon sinks/sources.

Products from the soil moisture and ocean salinity (SMOS) passive microwave satellite have proved useful in exploring AGC dynamics. The satellite delivers daily observations of L-band vegetation optical depth (L-VOD), a parameter related to the water content in vegetation mass. Previous studies have used L-VOD as an indicative proxy for spatially and temporally explicit assessments of AGC dynamics. The L-VOD-based AGC allows reliable estimates of annual net changes at regional or continental scales.

One challenge in elucidating AGC dynamics with coarse-resolution L-VOD products is to disentangle the interplay between losses and gains occurring in individual grid cells. For example, in $25\,\mathrm{km}$ L-VOD grid cells, the processes causing subgrid AGC losses (for example, local fire emissions) and gains (for example, forest recovery after disturbance) might offset each other within a year, leading to an ambiguous attribution of overall drivers of AGC changes. Further, both carbon losses and gains are influenced by local climatic, edaphic, biotic and anthropogenic factors. The processes underlying AGC change, especially those governing AGC gains, are not well understood, forming an obstacle to the effective management and conservation of tropical ecosystems in alignment with climate change goals.

In this study, we integrated passive microwave and optical satellite observations to create annual, wall-to-wall maps of AGC stocks and the corresponding change processes across the tropics for 2010–2020. Our domain covers most of the tropical regions, excluding Australia, and is further bounded by the extent of tropical moist forests (TMF). Our objectives are to:

1. Quantify the dynamic processes affecting the net AGC change in each L-VOD grid cell, specifically the gross losses and gains.
2. Investigate the factors governing AGC gains.

Combining L-VOD and optical satellite observations, we derived annual AGC net changes, gross losses and gains. Subsequently, we used a boosted regression tree (BRT) model to identify the key factors that govern the spatial variability and trends of the AGC gains. The novelty in our study is in:

1. The quantification of annual AGC dynamics over nearly all tropics.
2. The allocation of the loss to different processes (fire, deforestation and degradation).
3. An attempt to quantify the complex drivers of all types of AGC gains.

## Patterns of AGC loss

The gross annual losses in AGC from 2010–2020 were quantified at $-1.79\,\mathrm{PgC\,yr^{-1}}$. Fire emissions constituted the principal component of this loss, accounting for $62\%$ of AGC losses (or $-1.11\,\mathrm{PgC\,yr^{-1}}$). The magnitude of the non-fire deforestation and degradation losses is slightly lower but comparable to previous estimates.

Beyond this, our total loss considered the loss processes not only in forests but also in non-forested shrublands/savannas (inferred from fire emissions as these ecosystems are fire-prone). Overall, at the ecoregion scale, non-fire losses in moist broadleaf forests ($-0.55\,\mathrm{PgC\,yr^{-1}}$) and non-forest fire emissions in grasslands, savannas and shrublands ($-0.80\,\mathrm{PgC\,yr^{-1}}$) jointly accounted for $75\%$ of the tropical losses.

Over time, losses attributed to non-fire-forest disturbances, including deforestation and forest degradation, have shown an upward trajectory, increasing at a rate of $-0.36 \pm 0.12\,\mathrm{PgC\,yr^{-1}}$ per decade ($P < 0.05$). AGC losses due to fires, encompassing both forested and non-forested areas, showed no trend with fluctuations, with fire emissions of non-forest regions ($-1.08\,\mathrm{PgC\,yr^{-1}}$) being markedly greater than those from forests ($-0.03\,\mathrm{PgC\,yr^{-1}}$).

Regionally:

- **Tropical America:** losses from non-fire-forest disturbances ($-0.31\,\mathrm{PgC\,yr^{-1}}$) marginally surpassed total fire emissions ($-0.20\,\mathrm{PgC\,yr^{-1}}$), concentrated in the Brazilian “arc of deforestation”.
- **Tropical Africa:** loss hotspots are in savannas/shrublands. Fire-induced AGC loss in African non-forest biomes represented $77\%$ ($-0.80\,\mathrm{PgC\,yr^{-1}}$) of the total AGC losses in the continent ($-1.04\,\mathrm{PgC\,yr^{-1}}$). Non-fire-forest AGC losses totalled $-0.24\,\mathrm{PgC\,yr^{-1}}$ and were concentrated in moist forests (Congo Basin margins and West African coastal belts).
- **Tropical Asia:** smaller losses overall; non-fire disturbances accounted for $-0.14\,\mathrm{PgC\,yr^{-1}}$, slightly surpassing fire-related losses ($-0.11\,\mathrm{PgC\,yr^{-1}}$). Fire-related losses were mainly in mainland Southeast Asia; forest losses were more predominant in Indonesia.

Forest degradation induces AGC loss without producing discernable land cover change. In our estimates, degradation plays a slightly more influential role than deforestation in forest AGC loss. Across the tropical regions, forest degradation was responsible for $57\%$ of the total forest AGC losses.

## AGC net changes and gains

The estimated net change in AGC from 2010 to 2020 was $0.21 \pm 0.06\,\mathrm{PgC\,yr^{-1}}$ (mean $\pm$ s.d. of 18 estimates using different L-VOD AGC calibration). This indicates that tropical lands acted as a moderate carbon sink in AGC during the last decade. We observed a sustained carbon source from 2011 to 2016 (including the 2015/2016 drought), followed by carbon recuperation; the extended dataset shows AGC fully recovered by the end of 2020.

Upon deducting AGC losses from the AGC net changes based on L-VOD, we derived an estimation for the AGC gains. Across the entire tropics, the AGC gains represented a sink averaging $2.01 \pm 0.06\,\mathrm{PgC\,yr^{-1}}$ from 2010 to 2020. At ecosystem scales, AGC gains were highest in grasslands, savannas and shrublands (51% of gains; $1.02 \pm 0.03\,\mathrm{PgC\,yr^{-1}}$), followed by moist broadleaf forests (38%; $0.76 \pm 0.01\,\mathrm{PgC\,yr^{-1}}$).

AGC gains closely mirrored AGC net changes (correlation $r = 0.98$, $P < 0.01$), indicating that gains dominate interannual variability of net AGC changes.

Regionally:

- **Tropical America:** gains concentrated in the “arc of deforestation” and southern Brazil; $27\%$ of tropical gains ($0.54 \pm 0.02\,\mathrm{PgC\,yr^{-1}}$).
- **Tropical Africa:** predominant contributor; $58\%$ ($1.17 \pm 0.03\,\mathrm{PgC\,yr^{-1}}$), with key regions including Central African Republic, eastern South Sudan, Angola, Zambia and Tanzania.
- **Tropical Asia:** modest gains; $15\%$ ($0.30 \pm 0.01\,\mathrm{PgC\,yr^{-1}}$), notably Cambodia, central Sumatra and coastal Kalimantan.

Spatially, a strong negative correlation ($r = -0.82$, $P < 0.01$) between AGC losses and AGC gains implies that pixels with greater losses were typically associated with higher gains, consistent with post-disturbance recovery and regrowth.

Some regions may have underestimated AGC losses (for example, old-growth forests subject to degradation and drought), due to limitations in Landsat-based TMF data (missing small-scale degradation, cloud effects) and potential mismatches between L-VOD-detected losses and fire-emission accounting (for example, unburned coarse woody debris).

## Factors influencing AGC dynamics

The spatial variability of average AGC gain is predominantly governed by **fire radiative power (FRP)**, accounting for **>40%** of variable importance. Partial dependence indicates a near-linear dependence between AGC gains and FRP, highlighting the role of post-fire recovery, especially in African savannas and shrublands. The fraction of trees ($F_t$) is also important but much smaller (~9% importance). Young forest regrowth is influential due to rapid carbon accumulation, notably in West Africa, the Congo Basin and Indonesia.

For trends in AGC gains ($\delta$AGC gains), the change in **soil moisture** ($\delta$SM) is the principal determinant (about **10%** importance), with generally positive relationships between $\delta$AGC gains and $\delta$SM. Shortwave radiation trends ($\delta R_s$) are secondary; the absolute magnitude of $R_s$ also influences $\delta$AGC gains, predominantly negatively in some regions.

## Conclusion

Our research offers a nuanced spatial and temporal quantification of AGC changes over the tropics, detailing both loss and gain and associated governing factors. These findings enrich our understanding of aboveground carbon dynamics, highlighting the intricate balance between climatic, biotic and anthropogenic factors that regulate land carbon accumulation. These insights make it imperative to address the detriments of ongoing emissions to guarantee the longevity of carbon-rich ecosystems. Notwithstanding, the post-disturbance gains observed in certain locales offer a glimmer of optimism despite growing pressure on global biomes. We advocate for formulating and implementing effective policies and practices in land management to conserve indispensable ecosystem services.
